GDPR Data Breach — Preliminary Legal Advice
Document Type & Context

Preliminary legal advice memo prepared as part of the HSF / Clifford Chance / DLA Piper digital law virtual training programme, University of Bristol. Acting as legal counsel to Alpha Tech Ltd. following a cyberattack and data breach at its EU colocation data centre.

Executive Summary (§2)
"Alpha Tech may have good legal grounds to defend both claims. For the breach of confidence claim, Alpha Tech can argue that the data was not confidential or that Beta Corp failed to protect it. For the GDPR claim, Alpha Tech can argue that the data was anonymised or that it acted only as a data processor, following Beta Corp's instructions."
Key Arguments Developed
  • Patent documents are published post-registration and lose their confidential character — undermining the breach of confidence claim at its foundation
  • Alpha Tech's role as a colocation provider is distinguishable from a data controller under GDPR Art. 4 — processor status limits liability
  • If Beta Corp anonymised the health data prior to storage, GDPR does not apply — the threshold question of "personal data" is contested
  • Beta Corp, as a non-data-subject, lacks standing to claim individual compensation under GDPR — damages require proof of harm by the affected individuals

Art. 79 Exemption & Fundamental Breach under CISG Art. 25
Document Type & Context

Formal examination essay (3,978 words), University of Bristol LLM — International Sale of Goods (LAWDM0143). Written under assessment conditions; ranked in the cohort top 10%.

Question 1 — Art. 79 and the Scafom Problem
"The Belgian court's reasoning in Scafom conflates the CISG's exemption framework with the UNIDROIT hardship doctrine — two distinct legal instruments that operate on fundamentally different thresholds and produce different legal consequences."
  • Applies the four-limb Art. 79 test to an 80% coal price surge: foreseeability at formation is the critical limb — commercial price risk is typically foreseeable and assumed by the seller
  • UNIDROIT Principles apply only where parties have agreed to their application, or no applicable national law exists — they do not supplement CISG by default
Question 2 — Art. 25 Fundamental Breach
  • The "substantial deprivation" test under Art. 25 is assessed objectively, but courts diverge on how to weight the aggrieved party's legitimate expectations vs. the breaching party's foreseeability
  • Comparative analysis of German, French, and English case law reveals that the standard's flexibility produces unpredictable outcomes in practice — a structural limitation of the CISG

ICC Moot Court — Prosecution Memorial (Mege Case)
Document Type & Context

Prosecution memorial submitted in the ICC Chinese Moot Court Competition (national, 87 teams). Written as team captain and sole author; received the Special Contribution Award. Argued before the Appeals Chamber on charges of war crimes and crimes against humanity.

Core Arguments Summary
  • Jurisdiction: Art. 24.1 non-retroactivity does not bar ICC jurisdiction — Mege's arms supply was a continuing act extending into Ibein's post-ratification period (August 2020)
  • Crimes against humanity (Art. 7): Tawochi attacks were widespread and systematic, directed against a civilian population — "collateral damage" framing rejected; State-organised drone strikes constitute policy-based attacks
  • War crimes (Art. 8): Hospitals and schools used as pirate hideouts do not lose protected status — military necessity cannot justify intentional attacks on non-military targets
  • Art. 25.3(c) aiding & abetting: Supplying precision-kill drones with knowledge they would be used against civilians satisfies both the actus reus (substantial effect) and mens rea (awareness, not specific intent required)

Smart Legal Contract Analysis — MSC v CML & ATI
Document Type & Context

Internal advisory memo prepared for a senior associate ahead of a client call. Analyses a three-party smart legal contract dispute in the pharmaceutical supply chain — covering drafting defects, breach of contract claims, indemnity challenges, and SLC improvement recommendations.

The Oracle Problem
"The oracle problem is embedded in all SLCs: where a smart contract's execution depends on third-party data inputs, the security and authenticity of that oracle becomes a legal risk, not merely a technical one."
Key Arguments Developed
  • Drafting gap: Clause 6 Delivery Contract only activates on Docking Station rejection — since the tampered sensor showed compliant readings, ATI avoids liability despite probable transit spoilage
  • Independent breach route: Clause 3 Delivery Contract imposes a temperature obligation independent of sensor readings — MSC can pursue breach of contract damages on this basis regardless of Clause 6
  • Challenging the indemnity: "Found by CML" is entirely subjective with no reasonableness requirement; the 3-hour storage gap creates an alternative causation argument — damage may have occurred post-delivery
  • Reform recommendations: Align payment triggers with sample testing; introduce sensor certification; add objectivity and prior consultation requirements to indemnity clauses
